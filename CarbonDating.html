▼ For the curious: the science behind it

The Carbon-14 Clock

Carbon-14 is made in the upper atmosphere when cosmic rays strike nitrogen atoms. It mixes into the air as CO2 and is absorbed by every living thing. The ratio of C-14 to ordinary C-12 is the same everywhere on Earth and has been for thousands of years. When something dies, it stops absorbing C-14. The clock starts.

Remaining C-14 = 100% x (1/2) ^ (Age / 5,730)
After 5,730 years: 50% remains. After 11,460 years: 25%. After 17,190 years: 12.5%.

Why 5,730 years?

That is the half-life of Carbon-14 - the time it takes for exactly half the atoms to decay into nitrogen. This rate is set by quantum physics and cannot be changed by any amount of heat, pressure, or chemistry. It has been the same for billions of years and will be the same forever. That reliability is what makes it a clock.

Why can't we use it for dinosaurs?

After about 50,000 years - just 9 half-lives - the C-14 concentration drops below the detection limit of any instrument. For dinosaur bones (66+ million years old), every single C-14 atom decayed away long ago. Scientists use Uranium-238 instead, which decays much more slowly - half-life of 4.5 billion years - perfect for dating million-year-old rocks.

Independent cross-checks: Carbon dating has been verified against tree rings (going back 13,900 years), ice cores, coral, and written historical records. Every method agrees. Willard Libby invented it in 1949 and won the Nobel Prize in 1960.
How we date dinosaurs: Uranium-Lead dating of zircon crystals found in the rock layers surrounding the fossil. The crystal traps uranium when it forms. Over millions of years, uranium decays to lead. Measuring the ratio gives the age of the rock - and therefore the age of the fossil buried in it.
