Theory — How Planets Are Born

The Nebular Hypothesis

Our solar system formed about 4.6 billion years ago from a giant cloud of gas and dust — the solar nebula — which was mostly hydrogen and helium with traces of heavier elements forged in previous generations of stars. The nebular hypothesis, first proposed by Kant and Laplace in the 18th century and confirmed by modern astrophysics, describes five key stages.

Stage 1 — Nebula Collapse: Jeans Instability

A molecular cloud collapses under its own gravity when its mass exceeds the Jeans mass — the threshold where gravity overcomes thermal pressure. A shockwave from a nearby supernova likely triggered our nebula's collapse.

M_J = (5kT / Gm)^(3/2) · (3 / 4πρ)^(1/2)
k = Boltzmann constant · T = cloud temperature · G = gravitational constant
m = mean particle mass · ρ = cloud density
Lower temperature or higher density → smaller M_J → easier to collapse

Stage 2 — Disk Formation: Conservation of Angular Momentum

As the cloud collapses, any tiny initial rotation is amplified enormously — like a spinning ice skater pulling in their arms. The cloud cannot collapse into a point; instead it flattens into a spinning protoplanetary disk. Angular momentum L = m·r×v is strictly conserved throughout this process.

L = m · r · v = constant → v ∝ 1/r as r decreases
This is why the disk spins faster near the centre (Keplerian differential rotation)
Inner orbits: ~50 km/s · Outer orbits: ~5 km/s · Same as planets in our solar system today

Stage 3 — Dust Settling and Clumping

Within the disk, heavier dust grains settle toward the midplane and begin sticking together through van der Waals forces and electrostatic attraction. Millimetre grains grow to centimetres, then metres. This "planetesimal" stage (bodies up to ~1000 km) is the least understood — the "metre-size barrier" is a major open problem in planetary science, because metre-sized bodies drift inward toward the star too fast to grow further.

The Snow Line: At ~2.7 AU from our Sun, temperatures drop below ~170 K and water ice can exist. Beyond the snow line, solid material is 4× more abundant (water, ammonia, methane all freeze solid). This is why inner planets are small and rocky while outer planets became giant. Jupiter formed just outside the snow line where material was most plentiful.

Stage 4 — Runaway and Oligarchic Accretion

Once planetesimals reach ~1 km in size, gravitational focusing takes over — their gravity bends the paths of nearby particles inward, sweeping up material faster than a simple collision rate would allow. The largest bodies grow fastest (runaway accretion), then slow as they clear their feeding zones (oligarchic accretion). Each growing protoplanet dominates a region called its Hill sphere.

r_H = a · (m_planet / 3M_star)^(1/3)
r_H = Hill sphere radius (gravitational domain of the planet)
a = orbital semi-major axis · m_planet = planet mass · M_star = star mass
Earth's Hill sphere: ~1.5 million km (the Moon orbits well within it at 384,000 km)
dM/dt ∝ M^(4/3)  (runaway accretion phase)
Mass grows super-linearly — larger bodies grow proportionally faster
This produces a few dominant protoplanets rather than many small ones

Stage 5 — Final Assembly and Clearing

The last stage involves violent giant impacts between protoplanets — our Moon formed this way when a Mars-sized body called Theia struck the proto-Earth ~4.5 billion years ago. The young Sun's solar wind (T Tauri phase) blows away remaining gas, ending planet growth. Leftover material becomes asteroids, comets, and Kuiper Belt objects.

Giant Planet Migration: Jupiter and Saturn likely formed further out and migrated inward (then outward) in what is called the Grand Tack hypothesis. This scattered the early asteroid belt and may have delivered water to Earth via icy comets and asteroids — a crucial step for life.
Late Heavy Bombardment (~4.1–3.8 Ga): A gravitational instability among the giant planets — possibly triggered by Jupiter and Saturn crossing a 2:1 resonance — sent a wave of debris inward, bombarding the inner solar system. The lunar craters we see today are largely scars from this event.
