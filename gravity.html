Theory — What Is Happening Here?

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation (1687)

Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass. The force is proportional to the product of their masses and falls off as the square of the distance between them — the famous "inverse square law".

F = G · M · m / r²
F = gravitational force in Newtons (N)
G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²·kg⁻²  (Newton's gravitational constant — a fundamental constant of nature)
M, m = masses of the two bodies in kilograms (kg)
r = distance between their centres in metres (m)

This single equation governs everything from apples falling to galaxies colliding. In this simulation, it is computed between every pair of bodies on every animation frame (~60 times per second).

Circular Orbital Velocity

A body in a circular orbit moves fast enough that the gravitational pull exactly provides the centripetal acceleration needed to keep it on that circle. Setting F_gravity = F_centripetal and solving for velocity gives:

v = √( G · M / r )
v = orbital speed in m/s (shown as km/s in the readout above)
M = mass of the central body (the star)
r = orbital radius
Key insight: heavier star → faster orbits; larger orbit → slower speed

This is why Mercury (closest to the Sun) orbits at 47.9 km/s while Neptune (furthest) crawls at 5.4 km/s. The live readout above computes this formula in real SI units using your current star mass and each body's live distance.

Orbital Period — Kepler's Third Law

The time for one complete orbit follows directly from the circumference divided by the orbital speed:

T = 2π · √( r³ / G·M )
T = orbital period in seconds (converted to days or years in the readout)
Kepler's Third Law: T² ∝ r³  — doubling the orbital radius makes the period 2√2 ≈ 2.83 times longer
Earth at 1 AU around 1 M☉: T = 365.25 days ✓

The N-Body Problem

With two bodies, these equations give perfect analytical solutions — ellipses, parabolas, hyperbolas. With three or more bodies, no general closed-form solution exists. The system is mathematically chaotic: tiny perturbations grow exponentially over time. This simulation uses a numerical integrator — it steps the positions and velocities forward in tiny time increments, recomputing all forces at each step.

This is exactly how NASA computes real spacecraft trajectories and how astronomers model the long-term stability of solar systems. Over millions of years, gravitational perturbations in real multi-body systems can eject planets entirely — and evidence suggests our own solar system has done this in the past.

What Happens When You Change the Star Mass?

When you drag the star mass slider, the simulation immediately applies the new gravitational force. The existing planets' velocities are now wrong for the new mass — they were tuned for the original star. Orbits will destabilise. Drag the slider slowly and watch the planets spiral in (mass too high) or drift outward (mass too low). Hit Reset to respawn planets with the correct orbital velocities for the new star mass.
