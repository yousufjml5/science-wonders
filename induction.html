Theory — What Is Happening Here?

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Michael Faraday discovered in 1831 that a changing magnetic field through a conducting loop produces an electromotive force (EMF) — a voltage that drives current. This single discovery is the foundation of every electric generator, transformer, and motor on Earth.

ε = −N · dΦ/dt
ε = induced EMF in Volts (V)
N = number of turns in the coil — each turn adds to the total EMF
Φ = magnetic flux through one turn (Weber, Wb)
dΦ/dt = rate of change of flux — how fast the flux is changing
The minus sign is Lenz's Law (see below)

The key insight: it is not the strength of the magnetic field that matters, but how quickly it changes. A strong magnet held still produces zero EMF. A weak magnet moved rapidly produces a large EMF.

Magnetic Flux

Magnetic flux Φ measures how much of the magnetic field passes through the coil — like counting how many field lines thread through the loop.

Φ = B · A · cos θ
B = magnetic field strength in Tesla (T)
A = area of the coil loop in m²
θ = angle between the field direction and the coil normal
Maximum flux when field is perpendicular to coil (θ=0°) · Zero when parallel (θ=90°)

Lenz's Law — The Minus Sign Explained

The negative sign in Faraday's Law is not just mathematical — it encodes a deep physical principle discovered by Heinrich Lenz in 1834. The induced current always flows in a direction that opposes the change causing it.

Magnet approaching coil: Flux is increasing → induced current creates a magnetic field opposing the increase → coil acts like a magnet repelling the approaching magnet
Magnet retreating from coil: Flux is decreasing → induced current tries to maintain the flux → coil attracts the retreating magnet
Result: You always feel resistance when moving a magnet near a coil — this is where the energy comes from!

Lenz's Law is a consequence of conservation of energy. If the induced current helped the magnet rather than opposing it, we would get energy for free — a perpetual motion machine. Nature forbids this.

Induced Current — Ohm's Law

I = ε / R
I = induced current in Amperes (A)
ε = induced EMF from Faraday's Law
R = total resistance of the coil circuit in Ohms (Ω)
Higher resistance → less current for the same EMF

How Generators Work

Every power station on Earth uses Faraday's Law. A coil is rotated inside a magnetic field (or a magnet is rotated inside a coil). The angle θ changes continuously, so Φ = BA·cos(ωt) changes sinusoidally, and the induced EMF is:

ε(t) = N · B · A · ω · sin(ωt)
ω = angular velocity of rotation in rad/s
This gives alternating current (AC) — voltage oscillates like a sine wave
At 50 Hz (UK/Europe): ω = 2π × 50 = 314 rad/s
At 60 Hz (USA): ω = 2π × 60 = 377 rad/s
Transformer principle: Two coils wound on the same iron core. Alternating current in coil 1 creates changing flux → induces EMF in coil 2. The voltage ratio equals the turns ratio: V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁. This is how the National Grid steps voltage up to 400,000V for transmission and back down to 230V for homes — with almost no energy loss.

Real-World Applications

Electric generators: All power stations — coal, gas, nuclear, wind, hydro
Transformers: Every phone charger, power supply, National Grid substation
Induction cooktops: Rapidly changing field induces eddy currents in the pan — heating it directly
Wireless charging: Phone charges via induction between two coils
MRI scanners: Rapidly switched gradient coils induce signals in hydrogen nuclei
Metal detectors: Induced current in metal objects detected by secondary coil
Guitar pickups: Vibrating steel string changes flux in pickup coil → electrical signal
